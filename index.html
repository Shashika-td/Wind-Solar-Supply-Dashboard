Seasonal complementarity: Wind peaks in winter (Feb 47.3%) when solar is nearly zero (Dec 2.7%). Solar peaks in summer (Jun 20.5%) when wind is weakest (Jun 27.5%). The two technologies are anti-correlated by season — the core argument for a mixed power island at this site.

Worst wind month — June (avg wind CF: 27.5%, 10-yr avg)

Average hourly supply vs demand during the lowest wind month · solar compensates strongly in June

Wind (MWh/hr) Solar (MWh/hr) Combined (MWh/hr) Demand (MWh/hr)
Key finding — June (worst wind month): Wind drops to only 27.5% avg CF, but June is also the best solar month (20.5% CF) — solar peaks at 57.9% CF at midday. The combined supply curve closely tracks demand during daylight hours, demonstrating strong seasonal complementarity. Night-time remains wind-only, highlighting the need for battery storage to bridge the gap.

Worst solar month — December (avg solar CF: 2.7%, 10-yr avg)

Average hourly supply vs demand during the lowest solar month · wind is dominant in December

Wind (MWh/hr) Solar (MWh/hr) Combined (MWh/hr) Demand (MWh/hr)
Key finding — December (worst solar month): Solar contributes almost nothing — only 2 hours of meaningful output with a peak of just 15.5% CF at midday. However, wind more than compensates — December avg wind CF is 44.5%, one of the best months. At 30MW wind, output exceeds demand for most of the day, generating surplus for grid export revenue. This confirms wind as the essential backbone of the power island in winter.
